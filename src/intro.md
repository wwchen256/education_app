# What Are Morphemes?

Every word is built from pieces. Some pieces can stand alone — *run*, *cold*, *blue*. Others can't. You'll never say "flect" on its own, or "ject," or "re-" hanging in the air. But attach them to each other and something happens: *reflect*, *reject*, *inject*. The pieces have meaning. The combinations have meaning. And once you see the pattern, you can't unsee it.

Those building-block pieces are called **morphemes** — the smallest units of meaning in a word. This book is about them.

---

## Roots and Prefixes

Most of the roots in this book come from Latin or ancient Greek. They arrived in English through centuries of borrowing, mostly through French after the Norman Conquest, and directly from Latin in the Renaissance when scholars were naming new ideas.

A **root** carries the core of the meaning — the action or concept. {% root "ject", "throw" %} means *throw*. {% root "flect", "bend" %} means *bend*. {% root "port", "carry" %} means *carry*.

A **prefix** modifies that meaning, usually by adding direction, intensity, or negation. {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} means *back* or *again*. {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} means *down from* or *away from*. {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} means *into* or *inward*.

Put them together: {% word "reflect", "re-", "flect", "to bend back" %}. Or {% word "inject", "in-", "ject", "to throw into" %}. The meaning builds from the pieces.

---

## One Prefix, Many Roots

The most powerful thing in this book is not any individual word. It's this: **the same prefix works the same way across every root it touches**.

{% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} means *back* in *reflect* (bend back), *reject* (throw back), *return* (turn back), *recede* (go back), *recall* (call back). Learn the prefix once and it applies everywhere.

This is why this book teaches morpheme families instead of word lists. When you meet an unfamiliar word on the SAT, you won't remember its definition — but you might recognise its parts. That recognition is worth more.

---

## When Morphemes Change Shape

Here's something that will trip you up if no one mentions it: morphemes don't always look the same.

Latin had rules about how sounds combine. When a prefix ended in a consonant and the root began with one, the sounds often blended. {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} becomes {% prefix "im-", "into / inward" %} before *p* or *m* — which is why it's *import*, not *inport*, and *immerse*, not *inmerse*.

{% prefix "trans-", "across" %} sometimes sheds its *ns* — so *trajectory* is {% variant "trans-", "tra-" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}, meaning *to throw across*. The prefix is still there. It just changed its coat.

When this happens in a chapter, there will be a note. It's not an exception — it's a pattern. Once you've seen it a few times, you'll start to predict it.

---

## How to Read This Book

Each chapter takes two or three roots that happen to work well with the same set of prefixes, and teaches them side by side. The point is to watch the same prefixes do the same work on different roots — that's when the system clicks.

Read the chapters in order if you like structure. Or jump to whatever word puzzled you recently and work backwards to the chapter that explains its parts. Either way works.

At the end of each chapter there are a few exercises — words to decode using what you've just learned. No scoring, no tricks. Just a chance to see whether the pattern has landed.
