The root {% root "dict", "say" %} is Latin *dicere* — to say, to speak, to declare. It shows up in the most ordinary words (*dictionary*, *diction*) and in some of the most charged (*verdict*, *indict*). Saying has always been powerful.

---

## Saying: {% root "dict", "say" %}

Several prefixes you already know land on this root immediately. One new prefix — {% prefix "ad-", "toward" %} — arrives here too.

{% word "predict", "pre-", "dict", "to say before" %} — {% prefix "pre-", "before" %} from chapter 4. To say something before it happens. A prediction is a statement carried out in advance of events. *"The forecast predicted rain; by noon the forecast was right."*

{% word "edict", "e-", "dict", "to say out" %} — {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %} from chapter 2. To say something out — to pronounce it officially, publicly, with authority. An edict is a decree issued outward from power: a ruler's pronouncement, a papal declaration, an official order. *"The edict was posted on every city gate: no one was to leave after dark."*

{% word "indict", "in-", "dict", "to say upon" %} — {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} from chapter 1, but doing a different job here.

{% callout %}
**a new face of in-**

You've seen *in-* mean *into* or *inward*: inject (throw in), inscribe (write into), infer (carry into). You saw it mean *not* in *inflexible*.

In **indict**, *in-* means something closer to *upon* — to say something formally upon a person, to make an official declaration against them. To indict someone is to pronounce a charge upon them: their name is spoken into the legal record, with the weight of a grand jury behind it.

The same prefix, a third shade. Context — and the root it's attached to — tells you which sense is active.

*"The grand jury indicted three officials — said formally, upon the record, that there was cause to try them."*
{% endcallout %}

{% word "addict", "ad-", "dict", "to say toward" %} — {% prefix "ad-", "toward" %} is new here. *Ad-* means *toward* — it carries something in the direction of something else.

{% callout %}
**ad-** — toward

*Ad-* is a restless prefix. It assimilates to match the sound that follows it, so it shows up in many disguises:

{% variant "ad-", "ac-" %} before *c* or *q* — *accept*, *acquire*
{% variant "ad-", "af-" %} before *f* — *affect*, *affirm*
{% variant "ad-", "as-" %} before *s* — *ascribe*, *assert*
{% variant "ad-", "ap-" %} before *p* — *apply*, *appear*

The meaning stays constant: *toward*. Once you know the pattern, you'll see *ad-* inside words you've used for years.

In **addict**, the history is blunter than the modern meaning suggests. In Roman law, an *addictus* was a person formally decreed (*dict-*) over to a creditor — spoken toward another as property, assigned by official pronouncement. To be addicted, originally, was to be legally bound.

The word moved inward over centuries: from legal bondage to the bondage of compulsion. But the morphemes still carry the original weight. An addict is someone spoken toward something — bound to it, unable to turn away.

*"He was addicted to the work in the way some people are to a substance — the hours claimed him whether he chose them or not."*
{% endcallout %}

{% word "verdict", "ver-", "dict", "the true saying" %} — the prefix here is {% root "ver-", "true" %}, a new root element. *Ver-* means *true*: it appears in *verify* (make true), *verity* (truth), *verifiable*. A verdict is the true saying — the jury's official pronouncement of what is true.

{% callout "forms" %}
**prediction** (n.) — a saying-before; a forecast
**predictive** (adj.) — tending to say in advance; forecasting. *"The model was highly predictive — what it said before kept turning out to be right."*
**edict** (n.) — an official saying-out; a decree from authority
**indictment** (n.) — a formal saying-upon; the document charging someone with a crime. *"The indictment listed seven counts — seven things said formally against him."*
**addiction** (n.) — a state of being bound toward; compulsive dependence
**addictive** (adj.) — tending to bind; producing compulsion
**verdict** (n.) — the true saying; the jury's formal pronouncement
**diction** (n.) — the manner of saying; clarity and choice of words in speech or writing
**dictionary** (n.) — a collection of sayings; a record of what words say and mean
{% endcallout %}

## Old Roots, New Prefix

{% prefix "ad-", "toward" %} reaches back to roots you already know. In each case, *ad-* assimilates to match what follows.

{% word "admit", "ad-", "mit", "to send toward" %} — {% prefix "ad-", "toward" %} + {% root "mit", "send" %} from chapter 2. To send toward — to allow entry, to let something in. To admit a student is to send them toward the institution. To admit a fault is to send it toward the listener — to let it through. *"She admitted the mistake quietly, as if releasing something she'd held too long."*

{% word "ascribe", "as-", "scrib", "to write toward" %} — *ad-* becomes *as-* before *s*: {% variant "ad-", "as-" %}. To write something toward a source — to attribute it, to assign it. *"The painting was ascribed to a student of Rembrandt — written toward him without full certainty."*

{% word "adduce", "ad-", "duc", "to lead toward" %} — {% prefix "ad-", "toward" %} + {% root "duc", "lead" %} from chapter 3. To lead evidence toward a conclusion — to cite it, to bring it forward as support. *"The lawyer adduced three witnesses — led their testimony toward the argument she was building."*

{% callout "forms" %}
**admission** (n.) — a sending-toward; entry granted; also: a thing acknowledged. *"His admission of guilt came quietly, after hours of questioning."*
**admissible** (adj.) — capable of being sent toward; acceptable as evidence in court
**attribution** (n.) — a writing-toward; the act of assigning a cause or source
{% endcallout %}

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've now learned.**

**1.** *predictive* — from *pre-* + *-dict-* + *-ive*. A predictive model says things before they happen. How is this different from a *prescriptive* model (from *pre-* + *-script-*)?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

A predictive model *says before* — it makes forecasts about what will happen, based on patterns. A prescriptive model *writes before* — it sets out rules for what should happen, instructions in advance. Predictive: this is what will be. Prescriptive: this is what must be done. One describes the future; the other commands it.

</details>

**2.** *indictment* — from *in-* + *-dict-* + *-ment*. Why is an indictment different from a verdict?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

An indictment is a saying-upon: it formally declares that there is enough cause to bring someone to trial. It is not a judgment of guilt — only a pronouncement that a charge exists. A verdict is the true saying (*ver-* = true + *-dict-*): the jury's final determination of what is true. The indictment opens the case; the verdict closes it.

</details>

**3.** *admission* — from *ad-* + *-miss-* + *-ion*. It means both "entry granted" and "something acknowledged." How does "sent toward" explain both?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

To grant admission is to send someone toward the institution — to allow entry, to open the door toward them. To make an admission is to send something toward the listener — to release a truth in their direction, to let it through. In both cases something is being sent toward: a person, or a fact. The root carries the same motion either way.

</details>

**4.** *adduce* — from *ad-* + *-duc-*. A lawyer adduces evidence. What does "lead toward" mean in a courtroom?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

To adduce is to lead evidence toward a conclusion — to bring it forward as support, to present it so that it points toward what you're arguing. The lawyer leads the testimony, the document, the witness toward the verdict she wants. The jury is led toward the conclusion by what has been adduced. The root *duc* is doing its usual work: leading something somewhere with purpose.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
