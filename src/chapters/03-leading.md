Before prefixes touch it, the root {% root "duc", "lead" %} shows up bare: a *duct* is a channel that leads something through — air through a building, bile through the body, tears from the eye. A *duke* was originally one who led men into battle. The root is Latin *ducere*, to lead or draw. It leads toward you or away, outward or inward, depending on what gets attached to the front.

---

## Leading: {% root "duc", "lead" %} and {% root "duct", "lead" %}

The root appears as {% root "duc", "lead" %} in verb forms and {% root "duct", "lead" %} in many noun and compound forms — the same split you saw with {% root "mit", "send" %} and {% root "miss", "send" %} in chapter 2.

Two new prefixes arrive here. The first is {% prefix "pro-", "forward / forth" %}: *pro-* means forward, out in front. The second is {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %}: *con-* means together or with. Before *p*, *b*, or *m*, *con-* becomes *com-*: {% variant "con-", "com-" %} — the same assimilation rule you saw with {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} becoming {% prefix "im-", "into / inward" %}.

{% word "produce", "pro-", "duc", "to lead forward" %} — {% prefix "pro-", "forward / forth" %} + the root. To lead something forward into existence. A farm produces food; a playwright produces a script. What gets produced has been drawn forward — led out into the world.

{% word "conduct", "con-", "duct", "to lead together" %} — {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} + the root. A conductor leads the orchestra together, keeping every instrument moving as one. To conduct an experiment is to lead it through from start to finish. To conduct oneself is to lead your own behavior. The word does a great deal of work across very different domains, but the leading is always there.

{% word "reduce", "re-", "duc", "to lead back" %} — {% prefix "re-", "back / again" %} from chapter 1. To lead back to something smaller or simpler. A cook reduces a sauce by leading it back to its concentrated essence. You reduce an argument to its core claim, reduce a number to its simplest form.

{% word "deduce", "de-", "duc", "to lead down" %} — {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} from chapter 1. To lead your reasoning downward to a conclusion — to start with what you know and let the logic descend until an answer falls out.

{% word "induce", "in-", "duc", "to lead into" %} — {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} from chapter 1. To lead someone or something into a state. Warmth induces sleep. A persuasive argument induces agreement. *"Nothing he said could induce her to change her mind."*

{% word "educate", "e-", "duc", "to lead out" %} — {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %} from chapter 2. This is the most surprising one.

{% callout %}
**educate** — *e-* + *-duc-* = lead out

The word contains a theory of learning.

To educate is not to fill a vessel — not to push knowledge *in* (that would be *in-duce*: to lead into). It is to lead knowledge *out* — to draw from the student what is already latent within them.

This is the Socratic idea: the teacher does not deposit understanding; they ask questions until the student leads themselves to it. The best teachers have always known what the word says.

*"She didn't lecture. She asked questions until you educated yourself — until you led your own thinking out into the open."*
{% endcallout %}

{% callout "forms" %}
**duct** (n.) — a channel that leads something through; an air duct, a tear duct
**duke** (n.) — originally one who leads men; a title of military leadership that became nobility
**production** (n.) — the act of leading forward; something brought into existence
**producer** (n.) — one who leads a project into existence
**conductor** (n.) — one who leads together; also: a material that leads electricity through it. *"The copper wire was a good conductor — it led the current through without resistance."*
**reduction** (n.) — a leading back; a making smaller or simpler
**deduction** (n.) — a leading down to a conclusion; also: an amount subtracted. *"His deduction was clean: if the footprints led away but not back, someone had carried the body out."*
**induction** (n.) — a leading into; a ceremony of entry; in logic: reasoning from specific cases to a general rule
**education** (n.) — the act of leading out; the drawing forth of understanding
{% endcallout %}

## Old Roots, New Prefixes

{% prefix "pro-", "forward / forth" %} and {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} don't stay on *duc*. Go back to the roots from chapters 1 and 2 and watch them arrive there too.

{% word "project", "pro-", "ject", "to throw forward" %} — {% prefix "pro-", "forward / forth" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. You already know *ject*. A project is a plan thrown forward into the future. A projector throws an image forward onto a screen. A projectile is an object thrown forward through the air — and now that you have both roots, the whole family opens up.

{% word "conjecture", "con-", "ject", "to throw together" %} — {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. A conjecture is a guess thrown together from available pieces — evidence assembled without certainty. It is what you have before you can deduce. *"Without more data, everything we say is conjecture."*

{% word "convert", "con-", "vert", "to turn completely" %} — {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} + {% root "vert", "turn" %}. To turn something over entirely. To convert a religion is to turn your whole belief around. To convert a measurement is to turn it into its equivalent in another system.

{% word "commit", "com-", "mit", "to send together" %} — {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} + {% root "mit", "send" %}. To commit is to send yourself together with something — to entrust yourself to a course of action. *"She committed to the plan, knowing there was no going back."* To commit a crime is to send the act into the world; to commit to a promise is to bind your future to it.

{% callout "forms" %}
**projection** (n.) — a throwing forward; an image thrown onto a surface; a forecast thrown into the future
**projector** (n.) — a device that throws images forward
**projectile** (n.) — something thrown forward through the air
**conjecture** (n./v.) — a guess thrown together from incomplete evidence
**conversion** (n.) — a complete turning; a change of belief, unit, or form
**convert** (n.) — one who has been turned; a person who has changed belief
**commitment** (n.) — the act of sending oneself together with something; a pledge. *"A commitment is a sending — once made, you have dispatched yourself toward it."*
**committee** (n.) — a group sent together to handle a task
{% endcallout %}

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've now learned.**

**1.** *deductive* — from *de-* + *-duc-* + *-ive*. A deductive argument moves from the general to the specific. How does "leading down" describe that movement?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

You start with a general rule — all humans are mortal — and lead the logic downward to a specific conclusion: this person is mortal. The reasoning descends from the established to the particular. A deductive argument is one that leads downward from what is already known to what must follow.

</details>

**2.** *induction* has two very different uses: a logical one and a ceremonial one. What does "lead into" mean in each?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

In logic, inductive reasoning leads you into a general conclusion from specific observations — you notice that many things behave a certain way and are led into the belief that all of them do. In ceremony, an induction leads a new member into the group: a military induction, an induction into the hall of fame. One leads into a belief, the other into a role. The direction is the same in both.

</details>

**3.** *conjecture* — from *con-* + *-ject-*. What is a conjecture, and how is it different from a deduction?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

A conjecture is a guess thrown together — pieces of evidence assembled without certainty. A deduction has been led down to from established facts: the logic is tight, the conclusion follows. A conjecture is what you have before the evidence is complete: thrown together from what you can see, but not yet proven. *"The detective had a conjecture; she needed more evidence before she could make a deduction."*

</details>

**4.** *commitment* — from *com-* + *-mit-* + *-ment*. What does it mean to commit to something, in terms of sending?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

To commit is to send yourself together with something — to dispatch yourself toward it, to bind your future to it. A commitment is that sending: once made, you have launched yourself in a direction. To commit a crime is to send the act into the world; to commit to a person is to send yourself together with them. The word carries the weight of that dispatch.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
