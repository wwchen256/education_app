This chapter introduces one new prefix. But the root it arrives on is unusual — almost every prefix you've already learned attaches to it. For the first time, the whole grid fires at once.

---

## Writing: {% root "scrib", "write" %} and {% root "script", "write" %}

The root appears as {% root "scrib", "write" %} in verb forms and {% root "script", "write" %} in noun forms — the same split you've now seen three times: *mit/miss*, *duc/duct*, and now this.

The one new prefix is {% prefix "pre-", "before" %}: *pre-* means before, in front of.

{% word "describe", "de-", "scrib", "to write down" %} — {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} from chapter 1. To write something down — to capture it in words. When you describe a scene, you press it flat onto the page. A description is what language does to the world when it tries to hold it still.

{% word "inscribe", "in-", "scrib", "to write into" %} — {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} from chapter 1. To write into or onto a surface — carved into stone, pressed into metal. An inscription endures because it was driven in, not laid on top. *"The names inscribed on the memorial had been cut deep enough to last a century."*

{% word "subscribe", "sub-", "scrib", "to write under" %} — {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %} from chapter 2. Your signature went at the bottom of the contract — you wrote yourself under the terms.

{% callout %}
**subscribe** — *sub-* + *-scrib-* = write under

When you signed a contract in the ancient world, your name went at the bottom — beneath the terms you were agreeing to. To subscribe was to place yourself under, in writing.

The word has held its shape while the world around it changed. When you subscribe to a newsletter, you add your name beneath it. When you subscribe to an idea, you sign on to it — you place yourself under it, publicly. A magazine subscription is the same ancient act: you write your name under a promise of payment, and delivery follows.

*"He had subscribed to the theory for years — signed on to it so publicly that reversing himself now would cost him everything."*
{% endcallout %}

{% word "transcribe", "trans-", "scrib", "to write across" %} — {% prefix "trans-", "across" %} from chapter 2. To carry something across from one form to another in writing. A court reporter transcribes speech into text. A musician transcribes a melody from one instrument to another. The content crosses over; the root does the carrying.

{% word "proscribe", "pro-", "scrib", "to write publicly" %} — {% prefix "pro-", "forward / forth" %} from chapter 3. To write something out publicly — to declare it forbidden. In Rome, names were proscribed by posting them: written out on public display as condemned. The word has kept that sense of public declaration. *"The organization was proscribed and its members banned from holding office."*

{% word "conscript", "con-", "script", "to write together" %} — {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} from chapter 3. To enroll someone by force — their name is written onto the military roll alongside everyone else's. A conscription is names written together into service. *"Under conscription, no one could refuse — their names were already in the ledger."*

{% word "prescribe", "pre-", "scrib", "to write before" %} — {% prefix "pre-", "before" %} is the new prefix here. To write instructions down in advance, before they're needed. A doctor prescribes before you've filled the bottle; the written order exists before the treatment begins.

{% callout %}
**prescribe and proscribe**

One letter apart. Opposite in direction.

To **prescribe** is to write *before* — *pre-*. The doctor writes instructions ahead of treatment; the law prescribes what must be done. It sets down a requirement in advance, pointing forward.

To **proscribe** is to write *publicly, forward* — *pro-*. Something proscribed has been posted for all to see as forbidden. The law proscribes what must not be done.

Prescribed: required. Proscribed: banned. The prefix carries the entire difference.

*"The treatment was prescribed; the folk remedy was proscribed."*
{% endcallout %}

{% callout "forms" %}
**description** (n.) — a writing-down; an account in words of what something looks or sounds like
**inscription** (n.) — writing pressed into a surface; text carved into stone or stamped into metal
**subscription** (n.) — a signing-under; a recurring agreement. *"Her subscription had lapsed — her name was no longer written under the terms."*
**transcript** (n.) — a written-across record; a copy of spoken words or academic grades
**proscription** (n.) — a public declaration of prohibition; a ban
**conscription** (n.) — compulsory enrollment; the draft
**prescription** (n.) — written instructions issued before treatment; a doctor's written order
**scripture** (n.) — sacred writing; the root bare, no prefix — writing held as authoritative in itself
{% endcallout %}

## A Coming Prefix: ob-

One prefix to flag before it arrives properly. {% prefix "ob-", "toward / against" %} means *toward* or *against*. It already lands on a root you know.

{% word "object", "ob-", "ject", "to throw against" %} — {% prefix "ob-", "toward / against" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. To object is to throw resistance against something. An object is a thing thrown in the way — something standing there, in front of you, occupying space. *"She raised no objection — she threw nothing against it."*

*Ob-* becomes *op-* before *p*, by the same rule you've seen twice before. You'll meet it properly in the next chapter.

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've now learned.**

**1.** *transcript* — from *trans-* + *-script*. A school transcript records your grades. A court transcript records testimony. How does "writing across" describe what both do?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

A transcript carries something across from one form into another — speech into text, grades into record. What was spoken or lived gets written across into a permanent form. The transcript is the crossing-over: the original event on one side, the written record on the other.

</details>

**2.** *proscription* vs. *prescription* — decode both from their parts. What is the difference between something proscribed and something prescribed?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

A prescription is written before (*pre-*) — instructions set down in advance, pointing toward what should happen. A proscription is written forward publicly (*pro-*) — a declaration posted for all to see, naming something forbidden. Prescribed: do this. Proscribed: this is banned. The prefix carries the entire difference.

</details>

**3.** *conscription* — from *con-* + *-script-* + *-ion*. What does writing have to do with being drafted into military service?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Enrollment was an act of writing: your name was entered on the roll, written alongside the others. To be conscripted was to have your name written in — not by your own hand, but by the state's. The list was the power; appearing on it was the draft. The word remembers that military service began with a ledger.

</details>

**4.** *object* — from *ob-* (toward/against) + *-ject-* (throw). It works as both a verb and a noun. How does "thrown against" explain both uses?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

As a verb: to object is to throw resistance against something — to place yourself against a proposal or verdict. As a noun: an object is a thing thrown in the way, standing there against your path — something that exists independently, in front of you. In both cases something is placed against something else. The prefix does the same work either way.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
