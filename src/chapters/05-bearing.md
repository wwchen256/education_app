The root {% root "fer", "carry / bear" %} means *carry* or *bear* — the same territory as {% root "port", "carry" %} from chapter 2, but different in feel. *Port* tends toward physical movement; *fer* tends toward bearing something to someone, carrying something toward a recipient. The difference shows in the words.

---

## Bearing: {% root "fer", "carry / bear" %}

Two new prefixes arrive here. {% prefix "ob-/op-", "toward / against" %} means *toward* or *against* — you saw it previewed on *object* at the end of chapter 4. Before *p*, *b*, or *m*, *ob-* assimilates: {% variant "ob-", "op-" %} before *p*, {% variant "ob-", "of-" %} before *f*. The second new prefix is {% prefix "inter-", "between" %}: *inter-* means *between* or *among*.

{% word "offer", "of-", "fer", "to carry toward" %} — {% prefix "ob-/op-", "toward / against" %} shortened to *of-* before *f*. To carry something toward someone — to bring it to them and place it before them. An offer is what gets carried toward you; to offer is the act of carrying it.

{% word "refer", "re-", "fer", "to carry back" %} — {% prefix "re-", "back / again" %} from chapter 1. To carry something back — to send the listener back to a source. A reference carries you back to where the information lives. *"For details, refer to the previous chapter."*

{% word "prefer", "pre-", "fer", "to carry before" %} — {% prefix "pre-", "before" %} from chapter 4. To carry one thing in front of the others — to place it first. A preference is what you've carried forward, the thing you've put ahead. *"She preferred the second draft — carried it to the front without hesitation."*

{% word "defer", "de-", "fer", "to carry away" %} — {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} from chapter 1. To carry away — and from this one physical movement, two very different uses emerge.

{% callout %}
**defer** — *de-* + *-fer-* = carry away

The same root does two distinct jobs.

To **defer a decision** is to carry it away — to put it off, to push it down the road. The meeting is deferred; the problem is carried away from the present into some later time. *"The vote was deferred until more information could be gathered."*

To **defer to someone** is to carry yourself away — to step back, to yield, to let another person's judgment take the lead. *"She deferred to the surgeon on anything medical — she carried her own opinion away and let the expert speak."*

In both cases something is carried away: in one, the task; in the other, the self. The root is doing the same work. The context tells you which.
{% endcallout %}

{% word "confer", "con-", "fer", "to carry together" %} — {% prefix "com-/con-", "together / with" %} from chapter 3. To carry something together — to bring people into discussion. When a committee confers, its members carry their knowledge and judgment to each other. To confer a degree is to carry it together to the recipient in a formal ceremony.

{% word "infer", "in-", "fer", "to carry into" %} — {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} from chapter 1. To carry a conclusion into your reasoning — to bring it in from the available evidence. You don't observe it directly; you infer it. *"From the muddy boots and the early hour, she inferred he had been out since dawn."*

{% word "transfer", "trans-", "fer", "to carry across" %} — {% prefix "trans-", "across" %} from chapter 2. To carry something from one place to another. A bank transfer carries money across accounts. A student transfers credits across institutions.

{% word "interfere", "inter-", "fer", "to carry between" %} — {% prefix "inter-", "between" %} is new here. To carry yourself between — to get in the way, to insert yourself into something that wasn't yours to enter. *"He had a habit of interfering in conversations that didn't concern him — carrying himself between people who hadn't asked for it."*

{% callout "forms" %}
**offer** (v./n.) — a carrying-toward; something brought to another for acceptance or refusal
**reference** (n.) — a carrying-back; a source you are sent back to. *"The footnote was a reference — it carried the reader back to the original document."*
**preference** (n.) — a carrying-before; what you place first among options
**deference** (n.) — a carrying-away of oneself; yielding to another's authority or expertise. *"She spoke with deference to the elders — she carried her own certainty away."*
**conference** (n.) — a carrying-together; a meeting where people bring their knowledge to each other
**inference** (n.) — a conclusion carried in from evidence; a reasoned conclusion not directly observed
**transfer** (n./v.) — a carrying-across; movement of something from one place or person to another
**interference** (n.) — a carrying-between; unwanted insertion into something; also: disruption of a signal
{% endcallout %}

## Old Roots, New Prefixes

{% prefix "ob-/op-", "toward / against" %} and {% prefix "inter-", "between" %} don't stay on *fer*. They reach back to roots you already know.

{% word "object", "ob-", "ject", "to throw against" %} — {% prefix "ob-/op-", "toward / against" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. To throw something against — to interpose resistance. An object is a thing thrown in the way, standing there in front of you. *"No one objected; no one threw anything against it."* You saw this word at the end of chapter 4; now you have the prefix fully.

{% word "interject", "inter-", "ject", "to throw between" %} — {% prefix "inter-", "between" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. To throw a comment between others who are already speaking — to insert yourself into the middle of a conversation. *"She waited for a pause, then interjected before the argument could settle."*

{% word "opportunity", "op-", "port", "a carrying-toward" %} — {% prefix "ob-/op-", "toward / against" %} becomes *op-* before *p*, then lands on {% root "port", "carry" %} from chapter 2.

{% callout %}
**opportunity** — *op-* + *-port-* = carried toward

The word comes from the Latin *opportunus* — a wind that carried a ship toward (*ob-*) the harbor (*portus*). A favorable breeze. A moment when circumstances carry you forward rather than pushing you back.

The metaphor has never moved far. An opportunity is still that wind: a moment when something is being carried toward you, when the conditions are right to act. Miss it, and the wind shifts.

*"The peace talks were an opportunity — a wind carrying both sides toward the same harbor, briefly, before it turned."*

You've known this word for years. The root was there the whole time.
{% endcallout %}

{% callout "forms" %}
**objection** (n.) — something thrown against; a formal protest. *"The lawyer's objection was sustained — the question was thrown out."*
**objective** (adj./n.) — thrown toward the object itself; not filtered through personal feeling; also: a goal, something thrown out ahead to aim for
**interjection** (n.) — something thrown between; in grammar, an exclamation inserted into speech (*oh!*, *well!*, *ah!*)
**opportune** (adj.) — carried toward at the right moment; well-timed. *"His arrival was opportune — he appeared exactly when he was needed."*
**opportunist** (n.) — one who acts when the wind is favorable; sometimes used critically, of someone who acts only for personal advantage
{% endcallout %}

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've now learned.**

**1.** *deference* vs. *difference* — *deference* is *de-* + *-fer-* + *-ence*. *Difference* is *dis-* + *-fer-* + *-ence* (*dis-* means *apart*). What is the literal meaning of each, and how does it connect to what the word actually means?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

*Deference*: carrying away — you carry your own opinion away and yield to another. You step back. *Difference*: carrying apart — two things carried in different directions, away from each other. The literal sense survives in both: deference is about stepping back; difference is about diverging. When you show deference, you close the distance; when you note a difference, you name the distance.

</details>

**2.** *inference* — from *in-* + *-fer-* + *-ence*. How is an inference different from an observation?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

An observation is direct — you see it, you hear it, it arrives through your senses. An inference is carried in from evidence: you take what you can see and bring a conclusion in from it. The wet umbrella in the hallway is an observation; "it must be raining" is an inference — carried into your reasoning from what you observed. You didn't see the rain; you brought the conclusion in.

</details>

**3.** *objective* — from *ob-* + *-ject-* + *-ive*. It means both "not subjective" and "a goal." How does "thrown toward the object" explain both?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

An objective assessment is thrown toward the object itself — toward the thing as it actually is, unfiltered by your own position. Compare *subjective* (thrown under the subject, colored by your personal experience). As a noun, an objective is a goal thrown out ahead of you — something you aim toward. In both cases the direction is outward: toward the thing, not inward toward yourself.

</details>

**4.** *opportunity* — you now know both morphemes. A friend says "this opportunity won't last." Explain the metaphor hidden in what they just said.

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

An opportunity is a wind carrying you toward the harbor — *ob-* (toward) + *port* (carry, harbor). Your friend is saying: the favorable wind is blowing now, and it won't blow forever. Miss the moment and the conditions change; the wind shifts and the harbor becomes harder to reach. The urgency in the word was built in from the beginning.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
