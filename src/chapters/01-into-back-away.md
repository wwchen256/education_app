You already know what it means to **reflect** on something. And you know a mirror **reflects** light. These feel like different meanings — but they're not. Both come from the same place.

The root {% root "flect", "bend" %} is Latin for *bend*. The prefix {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} means *back*. A mirror bends light back toward you. When you reflect on a memory, your mind bends back toward it. Same word. Same logic.

Now watch what happens when you swap the prefix:

{% word "deflect", "de-", "flect", "to bend away" %} — *de-* means *down from / away from*. A shield deflects a blow; a clever answer deflects a question.

{% word "inflect", "in-", "flect", "to bend inward" %} — *in-* means *into* or *inward*. This one is less obvious. Keep reading.

{% callout %}
**inflect** — *in-* + *-flect-* = bend inward

The literal sense — bending inward — becomes clearer when you hear it in use. A speaker **inflects** their voice: it bends up at the end of a question, or curves down into a whisper. The voice bends *toward* what's being expressed.

In grammar, **inflection** means bending a word to fit its context — *run* becomes *ran*, *runs*, *running*. The word bends to match tense, number, or person.

*"Her voice rose at the end, the inflection of a question she wasn't sure she wanted answered."*

Also watch for: **inflection** (n.) · **inflected** (adj.)
{% endcallout %}

---

Now take a different root. {% root "ject", "throw" %} is Latin for *throw*. The same three prefixes work here too — and you already know what they mean.

{% word "inject", "in-", "ject", "to throw into" %} — to force something into something else. Medicine injected into a vein. An idea injected into a conversation.

{% word "reject", "re-", "ject", "to throw back" %} — to refuse, to send back. A rejected application is one that's been thrown back at you.

{% callout %}
**deject** — *de-* + *-ject-* = throw down

You won't often hear someone say "this dejected me." The base form *deject* has largely fallen from use. But its descendants are everywhere.

To be **dejected** is to feel thrown down — cast into low spirits. **Dejection** is that state of being. The logic is old and physical: grief as weight, as being flung to the ground. The same image lives in *depressed* (pressed down), *downcast*, *crestfallen*. Languages reach for this metaphor independently.

*"He sat in the corner, shoulders slumped, the picture of dejection."*

Also watch for: **dejected** (adj.) · **dejection** (n.) · rarely: **deject** (v., archaic)
{% endcallout %}

---

## The Pattern

The prefixes {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %}, {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %}, and {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} work the same way on both roots. That's not a coincidence — it's the system. Learn one prefix, apply it everywhere.

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've just learned. Don't look them up yet — reason from the parts.**

**1.** *trajectory* — comes from {% variant "trans-", "tra-" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. *Trans-* means *across*. What does *trajectory* mean?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

The path something takes as it is *thrown across* space. A ball's trajectory is the arc it traces in the air. {% prefix "trans-", "across" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %} = *to throw across*. Note that *trans-* has shed its *ns* before *j* — this is a Latin shortcut you'll see again.

</details>

**2.** *inflexible* — from {% root "flex", "bend" %} (a variant of *flect*) + *-ible* (capable of) + *in-* (not, when used as negation). What does *inflexible* mean?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Not capable of bending — in the physical sense (a rigid material) or the personal sense (someone who won't change their mind). Note: here *in-* is doing a *different* job than the *in-* meaning *inward*. Same spelling, two uses. Context tells you which.

</details>

**3.** *projectile* — from {% prefix "pro-", "forward" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. What is a projectile?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Something thrown forward — a bullet, a missile, a stone from a catapult. *pro-* is a new prefix here; you'll encounter it more in later chapters.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
