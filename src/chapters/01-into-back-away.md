You already know what it means to **reflect** on something. And you know a mirror **reflects** light. These feel like different meanings — but they're not. Both come from the same place.

The root {% root "flect", "bend" %} is Latin for *bend*. The prefix {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} means *back*. A mirror bends light back toward you. When you reflect on a memory, your mind bends back toward it. Same word. Same logic.

Now watch what happens when you swap the prefix:

{% word "deflect", "de-", "flect", "to bend away" %} — *de-* means *down from / away from*. A shield deflects a blow; a clever answer deflects a question.

{% word "inflect", "in-", "flect", "to bend inward" %} — *in-* means *into* or *inward*. This one is less obvious. Keep reading.

{% callout %}
**inflect** — *in-* + *-flect-* = bend inward

The literal sense — bending inward — becomes clearer when you hear it in use. A speaker **inflects** their voice: it bends up at the end of a question, or curves down into a whisper. The voice bends *toward* what's being expressed.

In grammar, **inflection** means bending a word to fit its context — *run* becomes *ran*, *runs*, *running*. The word bends to match tense, number, or person.

*"Her voice rose at the end, the inflection of a question she wasn't sure she wanted answered."*
{% endcallout %}

{% callout "forms" %}
**reflection** (n.) — the act of bending back; also: careful thought, looking back mentally
**reflective** (adj.) — tending to reflect light; also: thoughtful, given to meditation
**deflection** (n.) — the act of bending away; a turning aside of a blow, question, or criticism
**inflection** (n.) — a bending of the voice; also: grammatical change in word form
**inflected** (adj.) — modified by inflection; describing a language that changes word endings
{% endcallout %}

Now take a different root. {% root "ject", "throw" %} is Latin for *throw*. The same three prefixes work here too — and you already know what they mean.

{% word "inject", "in-", "ject", "to throw into" %} — to force something in. Medicine injected into a vein. An idea injected into a conversation.

{% word "reject", "re-", "ject", "to throw back" %} — to refuse, to send back. A rejected application is one that's been thrown back at you.

{% callout %}
**deject** — *de-* + *-ject-* = throw down

You won't often hear someone say "this dejected me." The base form *deject* has largely fallen from use. But its descendants are everywhere.

To be **dejected** is to feel thrown down — cast into low spirits. **Dejection** is that state of being. The logic is old and physical: grief as weight, as being flung to the ground. The same image lives in *depressed* (pressed down), *downcast*, *crestfallen*.

*"He sat in the corner, shoulders slumped, the picture of dejection."*
{% endcallout %}

{% callout "forms" %}
**injection** (n.) — the act of throwing something in; a shot of medicine
**rejection** (n.) — the act of throwing back; a refusal
**dejected** (adj.) — cast down in spirit; low, dispirited
**dejection** (n.) — a state of low spirits; despondency
{% endcallout %}

---

The third root is {% root "vert", "turn" %} — or in its alternate form {% root "vers", "turn" %}, which appears in many common words. Latin *vertere* meant *to turn*.

{% word "revert", "re-", "vert", "to turn back" %} — to go back to a previous state. A country that reverts to old habits. A wound that reverts to infection.

{% word "invert", "in-", "vert", "to turn inward / upside down" %} — to flip, to turn inside out or upside down. An inverted triangle. An inverted argument.

*De-* gives us a trickier case. {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} + {% root "vert", "turn" %} does not produce *devert* in English — the prefix shifted to *di-* (a variant of *dis-*, meaning *apart*): **divert** = to turn aside. This is one of those moments to note and move on — the prefix changed its coat, as they do.

{% callout "forms" %}
**reversion** (n.) — a turning back; return to a former state or practice
**reverse** (v./adj./n.) — turned back; the opposite direction or side
**reversal** (n.) — a complete turning around; an overturning of a decision
**inversion** (n.) — a turning upside down; a reversal of order or position
**inverse** (adj./n.) — turned around; the opposite or reciprocal
**inverted** (adj.) — turned upside down or inside out
**divert** (v.) — to turn aside; to redirect attention or traffic
**diversion** (n.) — a turning aside; also: an entertaining distraction
{% endcallout %}

## The Pattern

Three roots. Three physical actions — throwing, bending, turning. The same prefixes {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %}, {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %}, {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} thread through all of them, doing the same directional work each time.

That's the system. When you meet a new root, the prefixes you already know will be waiting there.

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've just learned.**

**1.** *trajectory* — from {% variant "trans-", "tra-" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. *Trans-* means *across*. What does *trajectory* mean?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

The path something takes as it is *thrown across* space — the arc of a ball, a missile, a career. Note that *trans-* has shed its *ns* before *j* — a Latin shortcut you'll see again.

</details>

**2.** *inflexible* — from *in-* (not, negation) + {% root "flex", "bend" %} (variant of *flect*) + *-ible* (capable of). What does *inflexible* mean?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Not capable of bending — physically (a rigid rod) or personally (someone who won't change their position). Note: here *in-* means *not*, a different job from the *in-* meaning *inward*. Same spelling, two uses — context tells you which.

</details>

**3.** *subvert* — from *sub-* (under) + {% root "vert", "turn" %}. What does *subvert* mean?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

To turn from underneath — to undermine, to destabilize from below. *Sub-* is a new prefix here; you'll meet it properly in the next chapter.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
