In chapter 1 you learned three prefixes — {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %}, {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %}, {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} — and watched them work on three roots. In this chapter those same prefixes return on two new roots. And three new prefixes join them. By the end, you'll have a set of six prefixes you can apply to any root you meet.

---

## Carrying: {% root "port", "carry" %}

The root {% root "port", "carry" %} means *carry*. It came into English through French, which took it from Latin *portare*. The meaning has never moved far.

Start with the most familiar: {% word "transport", "trans-", "port", "to carry across" %}. A transport ship carries goods across water. A transport network carries people across a city. The prefix {% prefix "trans-", "across" %} is new here — it will appear everywhere once you know it.

{% word "export", "ex-", "port", "to carry out" %} — {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %} means *out*. Goods carried out of a country for sale abroad. An exporter carries things out; an importer carries them in.

{% word "import", "im-", "port", "to carry into" %} — you already know {% prefix "in-", "into / inward" %} from chapter 1. Before *p*, it becomes {% prefix "im-", "into / inward" %} — the same prefix, slightly changed coat. {% variant "in-", "im-" %} before *p*, *b*, *m*.

{% word "deport", "de-", "port", "to carry away" %} — {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} from chapter 1. To carry someone away from a country by force.

{% word "report", "re-", "port", "to carry back" %} — {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} from chapter 1. Originally meant to carry information back. A reporter carries the story back to the reader.

{% word "support", "sup-", "port", "to carry from under" %} — the new prefix here is {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %}. Before *p*, *sub-* becomes *sup-*: {% variant "sub-", "sup-" %}. To support something is to carry it from underneath — to hold it up. This is the prefix you saw in *subvert* at the end of chapter 1.

{% callout "forms" %}
**transport** (v./n.) — to carry across; also: a vehicle or system for carrying people/goods
**transportation** (n.) — the act or system of carrying across
**export** (v./n.) — to carry out of a country; goods carried out
**import** (v./n.) — to carry into a country; goods carried in
**deportation** (n.) — the act of carrying someone away by force
**deportee** (n.) — a person carried away; one who has been deported
**reporter** (n.) — one who carries information back
**support** (v./n.) — to carry from underneath; to hold up, sustain
**supportive** (adj.) — tending to carry another's weight; encouraging
{% endcallout %}

The root also appears without a prefix. *Portable* means capable of being carried; *porter* is one who carries luggage or goods; *portage* is carrying a canoe overland between waterways. No direction added — just the carrying itself.

## Sending: {% root "mit", "send" %} and {% root "miss", "send" %}

The root {% root "mit", "send" %} means *send*. It appears as *-mit-* in verbs and shifts to {% root "miss", "send" %} in many noun and adjective forms. Same root, two faces — watch for both.

Before the prefixed words: notice *mission* and *missile*. No prefix — just the root on its own. A *mission* is the sending itself; a *missile* is something capable of being sent (*-ile* means *capable of*). The root stripped bare, doing the whole job.

{% word "transmit", "trans-", "mit", "to send across" %} — the same {% prefix "trans-", "across" %} from *transport*, now on a new root. A radio transmits a signal across the air. A disease transmits across a population.

{% word "emit", "e-", "mit", "to send out" %} — {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %} shortened to *e-* before *m*. A light bulb emits light. A volcano emits ash. The root is unchanged; only the prefix trimmed its final consonant.

{% word "submit", "sub-", "mit", "to send oneself under" %} — {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %} again. To submit is to place yourself under someone else's authority — to send yourself beneath them. A submission is what you send under: a manuscript submitted to a publisher, a wrestler submitted to a hold.

{% word "remit", "re-", "mit", "to send back" %} — {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} from chapter 1. To remit a payment is to send it back (to the creditor). The noun form, *remission*, carries the same root in its *-miss-* form.

{% callout %}
**remission** — *re-* + *-miss-* = sent back

A word that lives in two very different contexts but means the same thing in both.

In medicine, a cancer in **remission** has been sent back — pushed back into retreat. The disease has not been defeated; it has withdrawn.

In religious language, the **remission of sins** means those sins have been sent back, absolved, cleared away.

In finance, a **remittance** is money sent back — payment forwarded to a creditor or a family abroad.

The root *-miss-* is doing identical work in all three. The domain changes; the morpheme doesn't.

*"After two years of treatment, the scans showed complete remission."*
{% endcallout %}

{% callout "forms" %}
**mission** (n.) — a sending; a task or purpose one is sent to carry out
**missile** (n.) — something capable of being sent; a projectile
**transmission** (n.) — the act of sending across; a signal sent; a gearbox (sends power across)
**emission** (n.) — something sent out; exhaust, radiation, sound
**submission** (n.) — the act of sending oneself under; also: a document or work submitted
**submissive** (adj.) — inclined to place oneself under; yielding to authority
**remittance** (n.) — money sent back; a payment forwarded
{% endcallout %}

## Old Roots, New Prefixes

The three new prefixes — {% prefix "trans-", "across" %}, {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %}, {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %} — don't stay in this chapter. Go back to the roots from chapter 1 and watch them arrive there too.

{% word "eject", "e-", "ject", "to throw out" %} — {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. To throw something out. An ejector seat throws the pilot out of a stricken aircraft.

{% word "subject", "sub-", "ject", "to throw under" %} — {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %} + {% root "ject", "throw" %}. One of the richest words in this book.

{% callout %}
**subject** — *sub-* + *-ject-* = thrown under

The word carries the weight of its etymology in every use.

A king's **subject** is one thrown under his authority — placed beneath the crown. To **subject** someone to an ordeal is to throw them under it. To be **subject to** change means to be placed under its power, vulnerable to it.

Even in grammar, the **subject** of a sentence is the one placed under the verb — the agent that the predicate acts upon or describes.

In all four senses: under, beneath, subordinate. The prefix never leaves.

*"The prisoners were subjected to conditions that violated every standard of decency."*
{% endcallout %}

{% word "subvert", "sub-", "vert", "to turn from underneath" %} — {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %} + {% root "vert", "turn" %}. You saw this in chapter 1's exercises. To subvert an institution is to turn it from underneath — to undermine it, to erode it from below until it topples.

{% callout "forms" %}
**ejection** (n.) — the act of throwing out
**ejector** (n.) — a mechanism that throws something out
**subject** (n.) — one placed under authority; a topic placed under discussion
**subjection** (n.) — the state of being thrown under; subordination
**subjective** (adj.) — relating to the subject's own perspective; personal, not external
**subversion** (n.) — a turning from underneath; the undermining of an institution or belief
**subversive** (adj.) — tending to undermine; working against from below
{% endcallout %}

## The Growing Set

You now have six prefixes. Each one has appeared on at least two different roots:

| Prefix | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| {% prefix "re-", "back/again" %} | back / again | reflect, report, remit, revert |
| {% prefix "de-", "down from / away from" %} | down from / away from | deflect, deport, deject |
| {% prefix "in-/im-", "into / inward" %} | into / inward | inject, import, inflect |
| {% prefix "trans-", "across" %} | across | transport, transmit |
| {% prefix "ex-/e-", "out" %} | out | export, emit, eject |
| {% prefix "sub-/sup-", "under" %} | under | support, submit, subject, subvert |

Every new root you meet from here is a grid of familiar prefixes waiting to be applied.

## Exercises

{% exercise %}
**Decode these words using what you've now learned.**

**1.** *deportation* — from *deport* + *-ation*. What does it mean literally, and what does it mean in practice?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Literally: the act of carrying away. In practice: the forced removal of a person from a country, carrying them away from where they live. The morphemes make the legal violence of the word precise.

</details>

**2.** *emission* — from *e-* + *-miss-* + *-ion*. What is an emission?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Something sent out — light emitted from a source, exhaust emitted from an engine, sound emitted from a speaker. Note the *-miss-* form of the root (not *-mit-*), which appears in noun forms. Carbon emissions = carbon sent out into the atmosphere.

</details>

**3.** *subjective* — from *sub-* + *-ject-* + *-ive*. How does "thrown under" connect to a personal opinion?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

A subjective view is one thrown under the subject — filtered through your own experience, your own position. It sits beneath you, coloured by who you are. Contrast with *objective*: *ob-* means *toward/against*, so an objective view is one thrown outward, toward the object itself, free of the viewer's position.

</details>

**4.** *portfolio* — from *port* (carry) + *folio* (leaf, page). No prefix. What is a portfolio, literally? Why is that the right word for a collection of work or investments?

<details>
<summary>Reveal answer</summary>

Literally: a thing that carries leaves — the leaves being pages. A portfolio is a flat case for carrying papers or drawings. The word extended naturally: a portfolio of investments is the collection of assets you carry; a portfolio of work is everything you carry with you to show what you've done. The root earns its place — a portfolio is defined by what it holds and moves.

</details>
{% endexercise %}
